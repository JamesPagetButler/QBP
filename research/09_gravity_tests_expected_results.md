# Expected Results for Gravity Tests (Lensing & Rotation Curves)

This document outlines the real-world astrophysical observations related to Gravitational Lensing and Galactic Rotation Curves, establishing the "ground truth" for the ultimate test of our theoretical framework.

## 1. The Phenomena

These two distinct phenomena provide the primary tests for our understanding of gravity on cosmological scales.

### 1.1 Gravitational Lensing
*   **The Observation:** Light from distant galaxies and quasars is observed to bend as it passes by massive foreground objects (like another galaxy or a cluster of galaxies). This results in distorted, magnified, or multiple images of the background source, creating cosmic "arcs" and "Einstein rings."
*   **The Insight (General Relativity):** This is a direct confirmation of Einstein's General Relativity (GR), which posits that mass and energy curve spacetime, and light follows these curved paths. The amount of bending is a precise prediction of GR.
*   **The Insight (Dark Matter):** The observed lensing effect is often far stronger than can be accounted for by the visible matter (stars, gas) in the foreground galaxy or cluster. This implies the presence of vast amounts of unseen **dark matter**.

### 1.2 Galactic Rotation Curves
*   **The Observation:** Stars and gas clouds in the outer regions of spiral galaxies are observed to orbit the galactic center at a nearly constant, high speed, regardless of their distance from the center.
*   **The Classical Prediction:** Based on the visible matter in the galaxy, Newtonian gravity predicts that orbital speeds should decrease significantly as distance from the galactic center increases (similar to how outer planets in our solar system orbit more slowly).
*   **The Insight (Dark Matter):** This massive discrepancy between prediction and observation is the original and most compelling evidence for the existence of dark matter. The flat rotation curves suggest that galaxies are embedded in massive, invisible halos of dark matter that provide the necessary extra gravitational pull.

This is the final and most challenging test on our roadmap. It requires our quaternionic formalism to not only describe quantum mechanics but also the geometry of spacetime itself. Success is divided into two major goals.

### Goal A: Reproduce General Relativity

Our formalism must first prove itself to be a valid theory of gravity.

*   **Theoretical Challenge:** We must derive an equivalent of the Einstein Field Equations from our quaternionic axioms. Our theory must describe how the presence of mass/energy (represented in our formalism) affects the geometry of spacetime.
*   **Success Criterion:** Our theory must correctly predict the phenomenon of **gravitational lensing**. A synthetic test would involve modeling a massive object and a light path in our formalism. The model must show that the light path bends, and the amount of bending must match the predictions of General Relativity and astronomical observations.

### Goal B: Address the Dark Matter Problem (Aspirational)

This is the ultimate, revolutionary goal.

*   **Theoretical Challenge:** Can our theory explain the observed flat galactic rotation curves *without* invoking the existence of a new, undiscovered dark matter particle? This might occur if our quaternionic theory of gravity behaves differently from General Relativity on galactic scales.
*   **Success Criterion:** A synthetic test would involve modeling a spiral galaxy with its visible matter distribution in our formalism. The model would be considered a monumental success if it could naturally produce a **flat rotation curve** for stars in the outer regions, without the need to add an ad-hoc dark matter halo.

Achieving Goal A would prove our theory is a legitimate successor to GR. Achieving Goal B would solve one of the biggest mysteries in modern cosmology.
